Delivering the UX of automation to the traditional trade finance industry.

Among 50+ people dispatched within 5 scrum teams accross Europe, I joined the only designer at the time. I boarded the project when it had already some strong inertia (ongoing design debt) to manage. Catching up was done in a process of continuous improvement.

I bridged the gap between the product owner and the developers of my scrum team in the following ways:

  • Design for all user-flows during the KYC (Know Your Customer) process, from scratch.
  • Ensured consistency of interaction patterns across various documents libraries. Considering different use cases per libraries, within the platform.
  • Conceived implementation of web-based features adapted into an Outlook plugin.

But first, we need to understand the bigger picture: how global trade finance works, and where Blockchain technology comes into it wisely:

Komgo intervenes only in the financial aspect of the global commodity import/export activity. The other parts, focused on the supply-chain are managed by another platform called Vakt. The two platforms work alongside each others, a bit like the eBay/Paypal relationship and have been launched in sync.

Having this frame in mind and its level of complexity, we focused on prioritised smaller tasks with the Product Owner and the Business Analyst of my scrum team. We were designated to work on document management for KYC process, starting from the current state of the platform:

On top of the emptyness (and lack of empty states) of the platform, one of the most urgent improvement I made was upon the documents types and category distinction.

After a quick benchmarking of document management systems (among competitors within the same industry and more broadly) I drafted a lighter table to get rid of the generic accordion pattern used by default by developpers.

Users were aware of the kind of documents they were expecting and how to classify them, however they needed to find and manipulate them at a glance.

Going further in the document management enhancement, we took advantage of the power that a good filter tool can provide. To avoid re-inventing the wheel on this, we kickstrarted with the same structure as Stripe developped, and adapted it to our files classification.

This huge UX improvement wouldn’t have been possible without upstream of having hierarchized all operations-related types of documents within their relative categories. Sharing this schema with experts in their field let us streamline a UI on which all companies could rely on, as a standard.

This project drove me to produce user-flow charts on a regular basis to ensure a complete cover of edge cases. I also had the opportunity to work on some PRD (Product Requirement Document) in close collaboration with architects and business analysts to settle a design adapted to the technical challenges.

Here is a preview of a smooth workflow to enhance a basic feature: uploading documents in bulk.

I started by hand-draft ideas based on current modal in production, alongside having perfrom a benchmark of best practices.

After validation with the product owner and other designers, I re-used components provided by the front-end library.

Once most critical interaction issues were fixed and the design-debt cought-up, the backlog let us work on even better user experience, such as contextual empty states.

For this, I took in consideration the visual identity previously made by another studio, and played with abstract forms issued there to illustrate different specific context. Work on copywriting and call to action was also done.

For my last design task on this project, I’ve been asked to join another scrum team to embed features of the platform as a Outlook plugin. After discussions with the tech lead and product owner, I was able to be consistent with the web-based UI. Thanks to the Design system we put in place with the design team, the integration was straight forward and fast.
